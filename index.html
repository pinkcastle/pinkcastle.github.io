Radical new futures required radical new aesthetics


A hipster enclave adjacent to the big city, Pink Castle was long considered the "Brooklyn" of Mars. Dense with celestial van lifers and penniless academics, Pink Castle cultivated an ecclectic vernacular aesthetic distinct from other solarpunk futures. As part of the Royal Martian Academies' archeology enterprise, this exhibition showcases several furniture/design/media artifacts associated with the movement.

Geodesics


Geodesic domes offered the inhabitants of Pink Castle dynamic new spaces to cultivate a more human future. These Martians often used their geodesics for immersive 3D visuals, greenhousing, or fostering intimate spaces. The geodesic featured here, "DIWHI" (Dome Is Where the Heart Is), functioned as the centroid of the neighborhood and some archeologists speculate is why Pink Castle is called such. Others disagree, since the geometries of a dome are radically different than those of a castle.

Life on Mars

A cult favorite of Pink Castle denizens, “Life on Mars” is an interpretive music video of Earthian Seu Jorge’s variant of the Bowie classic. “Life on Mars” presents a historical perspective on social media from the early days of terraforming Mars. As the lead character bikes around the funky, artistic wilderness of Mars Beach, he remains absorbed in the scroll, missing the real encounter in exchange for its filtered, mediated counterpart. That is, until an obstacle quite literally becomes caught in the wheel of the social machine, forcing our protagonist to divert from his screen and the audience to inquire whether Mars is more than just a physical place.




Like many early off-grid solar punk communes, both Pink Castle and its neighboring boroughs dealt with the challenges of organizing waste. Indeed, early low-impact communities learning to live with the land and leave no trace grappled with the large amounts of waste their inhabitants made.
